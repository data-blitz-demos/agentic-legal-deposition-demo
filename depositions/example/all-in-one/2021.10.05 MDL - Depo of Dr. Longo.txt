 18   Could you just explain that a little bit for the 
 19   record so it's on the record what the method is?
 20       A.    Sure.  Both the CSM and the Blount are 
 21   fairly similar.  And the only difference is that for 
 22   the Blount method, it's specifically for amphibole 
 23   asbestos in Alice Blount's 1991 paper.  And so she 
 24   uses a heavy liquid that has a density of 2.81.  
 25   Therefore anything with a higher density, such as 

00047
  1   amphibole asbestos, from 3.0 up to about 3.2, will go 
  2   to the bottom of the test tube during centrifugation, 
  3   and everything else will be above it.  So there you 
  4   will just take the material at the tip of the 
  5   centrifuge tube and analyze it.
  6             The Colorado School of Mines is really just 
  7   a sample preparation method.  Alice Blount's is a 
  8   simple preparation method.  Then you have the other 
  9   tools to analyze it.  There we're using a heavy liquid 
 10   of 2.72, which will -- anything with a higher density, 
 11   such as amphiboles, et cetera, will go to the bottom 
 12   of the test tube.  The chrysotile has a density of 
 13   2.55.  Theoretically that goes to the very top of the 
 14   test tube.  And right under that will be the talc, 
 15   which has a density of roughly 2.66.
 16             And so you're using the same science.  The 
 17   science is well established.  It's just changing the 
 18   density of the liquid.
 19             The first recorded use of heavy liquid 
 20   density separation is back in the California Gold Rush 
 21   when they used the pans to pan -- people say they 
 22   panned for gold.  They take silt and dirt material 
 23   from where they think gold is with water and they 
 24   cause the water to -- it's sort of a sloshing motion 
 25   where the water goes over the top.  And then they add 

00048
  1   some more water and keep doing that.  Gold has a 
  2   density of approximately 14, and most of your silt and 
  3   dirt and fine-grain particles will have a density -- 
  4   fine-grain materials will have a density of about 2.6.  
  5   Water has a density of 1.1.  So they have to keep it 
  6   in suspension by sloshing it around in the pan very 
  7   carefully.  And over time all the silt and dirt and 
  8   whatever materials, except for maybe small petals, get 
  9   to the bottom of the pan, and they can pick out the 
 10   gold if they're lucky.
 11             Walter McCrone in his particle outlets books 
 12   goes through a series of densities, different 
 13   chemicals that have different densities to separate 
 14   different minerals.  Now, he doesn't use 
 15   centrifugation; he uses what's known as a settling 
 16   routine where the heavier density minerals, slowly 
 17   over time, will settle to the bottom of the test tube, 
 18   and anything that has a density higher will be above 
 19   it.
